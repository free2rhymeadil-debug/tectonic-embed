Rocks were given as return gifts in the girl’s school. It was the kind of “alternative” school where children were sent outside to “connect with nature,” even as the school quietly imported low-carbon sea sand from a beach 400 kilometres away. The parents bragged about sustainability on WhatsApp; the rocks didn’t mind either way.
The girl’s apartment complex was built on land that had once been full of rock formations. Entire hills had been blasted to make room for swimming pools, jogging tracks, and a clubhouse that promised “eco-conscious luxury.” Stray stones tumbled down the slope and she collected them like lost cousins.
After she received her first three stones she began testing the world: “Papa, is the floor a rock?” — “These are tiles.” — “But tiles were rocks?” — “Everything was rocks until capitalism polished it.” “Maa, is that on your finger a rock?” — “It’s a diamond.” — “Then I’ll ask for three diamonds at school.”
She dreamt she held the earth like a marble. She tossed it and it returned as rain; she swallowed one drop and felt the world inside her.
Across the city the banjara community had been displaced as townships arrived. The boy grew with a ruptured tennis ball and seven-stones games. Once a shard cut his knee; he pressed his palm to the rock and whispered “Sorry.”
They met under a Ficus elastica. “Close your eyes,” she said. She kissed his cheek. “I’ll tell Ms. Beena!” “Two Eclairs.” “Four.” “Two.” “Done.” They swapped aloo parathas and cake maggi. He flung one of her polished stones at the lake; it bounced three times and something inside her cracked.
As she grew, questions grew with her: Why mine forests? Why wall rivers? Why wall people? A culture is in trouble when it cannot answer what its children ask.
She joined protests. At a blast site she gathered three fresh fragments — warm, sharp, like the ones from childhood — and kept them in her palm.
After a protest, she walked past a glass-walled pub built on the hill his grandparents had once known. She threw a stone. The first spidered the glass. The second made a heart-shaped hole. The third slipped through and struck the waiter on the right temple. It struck him — the boy.
The next day the pub replaced glass with stone. Suddenly, rocks were “rustic.” Suddenly, displacement was décor.
Rocks remember oceans, volcanoes and winds. Water stones are still-motion; mountain stones are ek tarfa pyaar. Laterites show up late. Igneous stones are earth’s passion, cooled mid-thrust. Rocks are storytellers.
